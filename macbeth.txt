        <section id="introduction">
            <h2>Introduction</h2>
            <p>William Shakespeare's *Macbeth*, composed around 1606 during the Renaissance period (1500-1660), is a tragic drama set in eleventh-century Scotland and briefly England. Often called "The Scottish Play" due to theatrical superstitions, it explores the psychological descent of a noble warrior into tyranny, driven by ambition, supernatural influences, and moral corruption. The play draws from Raphael Holinshed's *Chronicles* (1577) but Shakespeare altered historical details for dramatic effect: portraying Duncan as virtuous (unlike Holinshed's weak ruler), making Banquo noble (to flatter King James I, who claimed descent from him), and emphasizing themes of regicide and witchcraft to align with James's interests in demonology and the divine right of kings.</p>
            <p>Influenced by the Gunpowder Plot of 1605, the play incorporates equivocation (deceptive language), referencing Jesuit plotters like Henry Garnet. First published in the 1623 First Folio, *Macbeth* is Shakespeare's shortest tragedy, blending history, supernatural horror, and psychological introspection. Its enduring relevance lies in its timeless exploration of power's corrupting influence.</p>
        </section>
        <section id="plot">
            <h2>Detailed Plot Summary</h2>
            <h3>Act 1 (Exposition and Inciting Incident)</h3><p>Opens with three witches chanting "Fair is foul, and foul is fair," establishing chaos and moral inversion. Macbeth and Banquo heroically defeat Norwegian invaders. The witches prophesy: Macbeth will be Thane of Cawdor and king; Banquo's descendants will rule. King Duncan names Macbeth Thane of Cawdor, fulfilling part of the prophecy. Macbeth informs Lady Macbeth, who vows to "unsex" herself for ruthlessness. Duncan visits their castle. Analysis: Introduces ambition's spark; prophecies tempt free will vs. fate debate.</p>
            <h3>Act 2 (Rising Action - Regicide)</h3><p>Macbeth hallucinates a dagger ("Is this a dagger which I see before me?"), kills Duncan offstage, and frames the guards. Lady Macbeth plants the daggers. Macduff discovers the body; Duncan's sons, Malcolm and Donalbain, flee, arousing suspicion. Macbeth is crowned. Analysis: Guilt emerges immediately; Macbeth's "sleep no more" soliloquy foreshadows insomnia motif.</p>
            <h3>Act 3 (Climax - Consolidation of Power)</h3><p>Paranoid about Banquo's lineage, Macbeth hires murderers to kill Banquo and Fleance; Banquo dies, but Fleance escapes. At a banquet, Banquo's ghost appears only to Macbeth, causing a breakdown ("Avaunt! And quit my sight!"). Lady Macbeth covers for him. Analysis: Marks Macbeth's isolation; ghost symbolizes unresolved guilt, shifting from internal conflict to public tyranny.</p>
            <h3>Act 4 (Falling Action - Prophecies and Atrocities)</h3><p>Macbeth seeks the witches, who conjure apparitions with equivocal prophecies. Macbeth orders Macduff's castle seized; Lady Macduff and her children are slaughtered. In England, Macduff joins Malcolm. Analysis: Equivocal prophecies build false security; massacre highlights descent into barbarism.</p>
            <h3>Act 5 (Resolution - Downfall)</h3><p>Lady Macbeth sleepwalks, scrubbing imaginary blood ("Out, damned spot!"), and dies (likely suicide). Malcolm's army advances, camouflaged with Birnam branches. Macduff reveals his caesarean birth and beheads Macbeth. Malcolm is crowned, restoring order. Analysis: Culminates in nihilism ("Life's but a walking shadow"); justice prevails, affirming divine order.</p>

        <section id="themes">
            <h2>Major Themes</h2>
            <p><strong>Ambition and Its Corruption:</strong> Central theme. Unchecked desire destroys morality. Prophecies ignite Macbeth's "vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself."</p>
            <p><strong>Guilt and Conscience:</strong> Psychological torment post-crime. Manifests in Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking and Macbeth's hallucinations.</p>
            <p><strong>Fate vs. Free Will:</strong> Do prophecies predetermine events or merely tempt human action? Equivocation suggests human agency is key.</p>
            <p><strong>Appearance vs. Reality:</strong> Deception abounds ("fair is foul"). Macbeth's honorable facade hides tyranny. "There's daggers in men's smiles."</p>
            <p><strong>Violence and Masculinity:</strong> Equates manhood with brutality. Lady Macbeth's "unsex me here" rejects traditional femininity for cruelty.</p>
            <p><strong>Kingship and Tyranny:</strong> Contrasts legitimate, divine rule (Duncan/Malcolm) with usurpation. Tyranny leads to isolation and downfall.</p>
        </section>
        <section id="motifs">
            <h2>Motifs and Symbols</h2>
            <p><strong>Blood:</strong> Symbolizes guilt. Evolves from an image of valor ("smoked with bloody execution") to a persistent stain of sin ("Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?").</p>
            <p><strong>Sleep:</strong> Represents innocence and peace of mind. After murdering Duncan, Macbeth "does murder sleep," leading to his and Lady Macbeth's torment and insomnia.</p>
            <p><strong>Darkness & Night:</strong> Conceals evil deeds. Murders and plotting occur at night, reflecting the moral darkness of the characters.</p>
            <p><strong>Unnatural Events:</strong> Storms, earthquakes, and animal behavior (a falcon killed by an owl) reflect the disruption of the natural and divine order caused by regicide.</p>
        </section>

        <section id="interpretations">
            <h2>Critical Interpretations</h2>
            <p><strong>Psychoanalytic (Freud):</strong> Reads the play through the lens of repressed desires and guilt. Macbeth's hallucinations (dagger, ghost) are seen as projections of his guilty superego. Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is the return of the repressed.</p>
            <p><strong>Feminist (Janet Adelman):</strong> Argues that the play is driven by male anxiety about female power. Lady Macbeth subverts traditional gender roles but is ultimately punished with madness and death, reinforcing patriarchal norms.</p>
            <p><strong>New Historicist (Stephen Greenblatt):</strong> Connects the play to its specific historical context—Jacobean England's fear of regicide after the Gunpowder Plot, King James I's interest in witchcraft, and the politics of equivocation.</p>
            <p><strong>A.C. Bradley (Tragic Hero):</strong> Views Macbeth as a classic tragic hero whose noble qualities are destroyed by a single fatal flaw ("vaulting ambition"), leading to his inevitable downfall.</p>

                    <h3>Improved Version</h3>
                    <p>In *Macbeth*, Shakespeare dramatizes ambition as a corrupting force that erodes moral identity, transforming a valiant warrior into a nihilistic tyrant. Initially, Macbeth’s "vaulting ambition" is not an inherent evil but a latent potential, ignited by the witches’ equivocal prophecies. His aside, "Two truths are told / As happy prologues to the swelling act," employs a theatrical metaphor, revealing his view of destiny as a script he can now enact. This desire is weaponized by Lady Macbeth, who rejects her femininity to embrace "direst cruelty," equating power with a violent masculinity. However, the play argues that ambition divorced from morality is self-destructive. The psychological torment of both characters—manifested in hallucinations and somnambulism—culminates in Macbeth's "Tomorrow, and tomorrow" soliloquy, where his once-great aspirations collapse into the revelation that life is a "tale told by an idiot, signifying nothing." Ultimately, Shakespeare presents a cautionary tale for his Jacobean audience, asserting that ambition without a moral compass leads not to glory, but to spiritual and literal annihilation.</p>

        let currentQuestion = 0; let score = 0;
        const quizContainer = document.getElementById('quiz-container');
        const scoreEl = document.getElementById('quizScore');
        const progressFill = document.getElementById('progressFill');

        function loadQuiz() {
            quizContainer.innerHTML = '';
            const qData = quizData[currentQuestion];
            const questionEl = document.createElement('div');
            questionEl.className = 'quiz-question';
            questionEl.innerHTML = `<h4>Question ${currentQuestion + 1}: ${qData.q}</h4><div class="quiz-options"></div>`;
            const optionsEl = questionEl.querySelector('.quiz-options');
            qData.o.forEach(optionText => {
                const label = document.createElement('label');
                label.className = 'quiz-option';
                label.textContent = optionText;
                label.onclick = () => selectAnswer(label, optionText, qData.a);
                optionsEl.appendChild(label);
            });
            quizContainer.appendChild(questionEl);
        }
        function selectAnswer(labelEl, selected, correct) {
            document.querySelectorAll('.quiz-option').forEach(opt => opt.onclick = null); // Disable options
            if (selected === correct) {
                labelEl.classList.add('correct');
                score++;
            } else {
                labelEl.classList.add('incorrect');
                document.querySelectorAll('.quiz-option').forEach(opt => { if (opt.textContent === correct) opt.classList.add('correct'); });
            }
            currentQuestion++;
            scoreEl.textContent = score;
            progressFill.style.width = `${(currentQuestion / quizData.length) * 100}%`;
            setTimeout(() => {
                if (currentQuestion < quizData.length) loadQuiz();
                else quizContainer.innerHTML = `<h4>Quiz Complete! Your final score is ${score}/${quizData.length}.</h4>`;
            }, 1500);
        }
        document.addEventListener('DOMContentLoaded', loadQuiz);

        // Essay Analyzer Logic
        const weakWords = ['very','really','thing','things','a lot','good','bad','stuff','nice','okay','ok','literally','maybe','so','just','actually'];
        const analyticVerbs = ['shows','demonstrates','suggests','reveals','indicates','illustrates','underscores','highlights','exposes','symbolizes','represents','implies','conveys'];
        const themeWords = ['ambition','guilt','fate','prophecy','supernatural','sleep','blood','tyranny','power','kingship','masculinity','gender'];
        
        function splitSentences(text) { return text.replace(/([.?!])\s*(?=[A-Z])/g, "$1|").split("|").map(s => s.trim()).filter(Boolean); }

        function scoreSentence(s) {
            let score = 0; const tips = []; const lower = s.toLowerCase();
            if (/["'“”‘’]/.test(s)) { score += 2; tips.push('✓ Contains a quote.'); }
            if (analyticVerbs.some(v => lower.includes(' ' + v))) { score += 1; tips.push('✓ Uses analytical language.'); }
            if (themeWords.some(w => lower.includes(w))) { score += 1; tips.push('✓ Links to a key theme.'); }
            const foundWeak = weakWords.filter(w => lower.match(new RegExp('\\b' + w + '\\b')));
            if (foundWeak.length) { score -= 2; tips.push('✗ Contains weak word(s): ' + [...new Set(foundWeak)].join(', ')); }
            if (s.split(/\s+/).length < 6) { score -= 1; tips.push('✗ Very short sentence.'); }
            return {score, tips, foundWeak};
        }

        function analyzeEssay() {
            const raw = document.getElementById('essayInput').value.trim();
            if (!raw) { document.getElementById('previewArea').textContent = 'Please enter an essay to analyze.'; return; }
            const sents = splitSentences(raw);
            const preview = document.getElementById('previewArea');
            preview.innerHTML = '';
            sents.forEach(s => {
                const res = scoreSentence(s);
                let html = s;
                if (res.foundWeak.length) {
                    [...new Set(res.foundWeak)].forEach(w => {
                        html = html.replace(new RegExp('\\b' + w + '\\b', 'ig'), `<mark class="weak tooltip" data-tip="Weak word. Try being more specific.">${w}</mark>`);
                    });
                }
                html = html.replace(/(["'“”‘’][^"'“”‘’]{10,}?["'“”‘’])/g, '<mark class="quote tooltip" data-tip="Good evidence. Ensure you explain its significance.">$1</mark>');
                const span = document.createElement('span');
                let status = res.score >= 2 ? 'good' : res.score <= -1 ? 'bad' : 'meh';
                span.className = `sentence tooltip ${status}`;
                span.innerHTML = html + ' ';
                span.setAttribute('data-tip', `Score: ${res.score}\n${res.tips.join('\n')}`);
                preview.appendChild(span);
            });
        }
        function useSampleEssay() {
            document.getElementById('essayInput').value = `Macbeth's ambition is a very destructive force. Shakespeare really shows this when Macbeth says "To be thus is nothing, but to be safely thus." This quote is good because it reveals his paranoia. Lady Macbeth is also a bad person. She is maybe more ambitious than him. In the end, everything is just tragic.`;
            analyzeEssay();
        }
